Taxonomy of bilevel problems

Bilevel optimization (BLO) problems can be categorized using structural and behavioral characteristics [1]. This taxonomy is useful for (i) selecting algorithms, (ii) anticipating computational difficulty, and (iii) reporting modeling assumptions (e.g., optimistic vs pessimistic) clearly.

  • Based on number of followers: This category specifies how many lower-level decision-makers respond to a single upper-level leader.
    • (i) Single-follower BLO: A single follower solves one lower-level optimization problem in response to the leader (the most commonly studied case):
      \begin{equation}\label{eq:tax_single_follower} \begin{aligned} &\min_{\mathbf{x}_u,\mathbf{x}_l}\, F(\mathbf{x}_u,\mathbf{x}_l)\\ & \text{s.t.} \begin{cases} G(\mathbf{x}_u\mathbf{x}_l)\le 0,\\ \mathbf{x}_l\in \arg\!\min_{\mathbf{x}_l} \left\{ f(\mathbf{x}_u,\mathbf{x}_l) \ \big|\ g(\mathbf{x}_u,\mathbf{x}_l)\le 0 \right\}.\end{cases} \end{aligned} \end{equation}

      Interpretation: the leader anticipates the follower's best response \(\mathbf{x}_l^*\) .
      Where it appears: leader–follower games, pricing, interdiction, and ML tasks (e.g., hyperparameter tuning as upper-level and training as lower-level).
      Method implications: KKT/duality or differentiable BLO is often viable when the lower-level is convex/smooth; nested or derivative-free methods are used for discrete/nonconvex/black-box lower-level.

    • (ii) Multi-follower BLO: Multiple followers solve lower-level problems, either independently or with interactions (markets, decentralized control, multi-agent systems) [2], [3].

      Independent followers (separable lower-levels):

      \begin{equation}\label{eq:tax_multi_follower_ind} \begin{aligned} &\min_{\mathbf{x}_u} F(\mathbf{x}_u,\mathbf{x}_{l_1},\ldots,\mathbf{x}_{l_m})\\ &\text{s.t.}\begin{cases} G(\mathbf{x}_u,\,\mathbf{x}_l)\le 0,\\ \mathbf{x}_{l_i} \in \arg\!\min_{\mathbf{x}_{l_i}}\left\{ f_i(\mathbf{x}_u,\mathbf{x}_{l_i})\ \big|\ g_i(\mathbf{x}_u,\mathbf{x}_{l_i})\le 0 \right\}, \quad i=1,\ldots,m.\end{cases} \end{aligned} \end{equation}

      Interpretation: each follower reacts separately; evaluation typically requires \(m\) lower-level solves per upper-level candidate.

      Interacting followers (Generalized Nash / equilibrium-type lower-level):

      \begin{equation}\label{eq:tax_multi_follower_int} \begin{aligned} &\min_{\mathbf{x}_u} F(\mathbf{x}_u,\mathbf{x}_{l_1},\ldots,\mathbf{x}_{l_m})\\ &\text{s.t.}\begin{cases} G(\mathbf{x}_u,\,\mathbf{x}_l)\le 0,\\ \mathbf{x}_{l_i} \in \arg\!\min_{\mathbf{x}_{l_i}\in \mathbf{X}_{l_i}(\mathbf{x}_u,\mathbf{x}_{l_{-i}})} f_i(\mathbf{x}_u,\mathbf{x}_{l_i},\mathbf{x}_{l_{-i}})\\ g_i(\mathbf{x}_u,\mathbf{x}_{l_i},\mathbf{x}_{l_{-i}})\le 0, \quad i=1,\ldots,m,\end{cases} \end{aligned}\end{equation}

      where \(\mathbf{x}_{l_{-i}}=(\mathbf{x}_{l_1},\ldots,\mathbf{x}_{l_{i-1}},\mathbf{x}_{l_{i+1}},\ldots,\mathbf{x}_{l_m})\).
      Interpretation: the lower-level outcome is an equilibrium concept rather than independent optima.
      Method implications: decomposition/penalty/equilibrium reformulations are common; uniqueness/existence assumptions matter more.

  • Based on coupling structure: Coupling describes how upper-level and lower-level constraints depend on each other’s variables, strongly influencing tractability.
    • (i) Decoupled constraints: The upper-level constraints involve only \(\mathbf{x}_u\) and lower-level constraints involve only \(\mathbf{x}_l\), while objectives may remain coupled:
      \begin{equation}\label{eq:tax_decoupled} \begin{aligned} &\min_{\mathbf{x}_u,\mathbf{x}_l} F(\mathbf{x}_u,\mathbf{x}_l)\\ &\text{s.t.}\begin{cases} G(\mathbf{x}_u)\le 0,\\ \mathbf{x}_l\in \arg\min_{\mathbf{x}_l} \left\{ f(\mathbf{x}_u,\mathbf{x}_l)\ \big|\ g(\mathbf{x}_l)\le 0 \right\}.\end{cases} \end{aligned} \end{equation}

      Interpretation: feasibility sets are structurally simpler, but bilevel difficulty persists via the lower-level optimality mapping.
      Method implications: lower-level feasibility does not change with \(\mathbf{x}_u\); however \(\mathbf{x}_u\mapsto \mathbf{x}_l^*(\mathbf{x}_u)\) can stilower-level be nonsmooth or set-valued.

    • (ii) Fully coupled constraints: UL and lower-level constraints share variables (the most general and often most realistic case) [2], [4]:
      \begin{equation}\label{eq:tax_fully_coupled} \begin{aligned} \min_{\mathbf{x},\mathbf{x}_l} \quad & F(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{x}_l)\\ \text{s.t.}\quad & G(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{x}_l)\le 0,\\ & \mathbf{x}_l\in \arg\min_{\mathbf{v}} \left\{ f(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{v})\ \big|\ g(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{v})\le 0 \right\}. \end{aligned} \end{equation}

      Interpretation: \(\mathbf{x}\) can alter lower-level feasibility and the lower-level optimum set; regime switches are common.
      Method implications: KKT/MPEC reformulations often lead to complementarity constraints; discrete/nonconvex lower-level usually requires specialized or heuristic approaches.

  • Based on number of criteria in lower-level (multiobjective LL): If the lower-level has multiple conflicting objectives, the lower-level “solution” becomes a Pareto-optimal set [5].
    \begin{equation}\label{eq:tax_multiobj_LL} \begin{aligned} \min_{\mathbf{x},\mathbf{x}_l} \quad & F(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{x}_l)\\ \text{s.t.}\quad & \mathbf{x}_l\in \mathcal{P}(\mathbf{x}) := \left\{ \mathbf{x}_l\in\mathbf{x}_l\ \big|\ \mathbf{x}_l\ \text{is Pareto-optimal for}\ \min_{\mathbf{v}\in\mathbf{x}_l} \ \mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{v})\right\}. \end{aligned} \end{equation}

    Interpretation: the lower-level reaction is a set \(\mathcal{P}(\mathbf{x})\); a selection rule is needed to evaluate the UL objective.
    Why challenging: it is not known a priori which lower-level Pareto points induce UL optimality; algorithms may need a diverse lower-level Pareto approximation.
    Method implications: must define (and report) lower-level preference/selection (scalarization, reference point, representative set, etc.).

  • Based on lower-level model selection (tie-breaking): When the lower-level has multiple optimal solutions, the modeling choice defines tie-breaking. Let \[ S(\mathbf{x}) := \arg\min_{\mathbf{v}} \left\{ f(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{v}) \ \big|\ g(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{v})\le 0 \right\}. \]
    • (i) Optimistic bilevel:
      \begin{equation}\label{eq:tax_optimistic} \begin{aligned} \min_{\mathbf{x},\mathbf{x}_l} \quad & F(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{x}_l)\\ \text{s.t.}\quad & G(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{x}_l)\le 0,\\ & \mathbf{x}_l\in S(\mathbf{x}). \end{aligned} \end{equation}

      Interpretation: if \(S(\mathbf{x})\) is multi-valued, the follower selects an LL-optimal \(\mathbf{x}_l\) most favorable to the leader.
      Method implications: often more tractable; widely used in benchmarks and algorithm development.

    • (ii) Pessimistic bilevel:
      \begin{equation}\label{eq:tax_pessimistic} \begin{aligned} \min_{\mathbf{x}} \ \max_{\mathbf{x}_l\in S(\mathbf{x})} \quad & F(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{x}_l)\\ \text{s.t.}\quad & \mathbf{x}\in \mathbf{X}. \end{aligned} \end{equation}

      Interpretation: the leader plans against the worst LL-optimal response [6].
      Method implications: typically harder; solutions may be sensitive to how the inner maximization over \(S(\mathbf{x})\) is computed.

  • Based on determinism (deterministic / stochastic / robust): This category specifies whether parameters are known exactly or uncertain [7], [8].
    • (i) Deterministic BLO:
      \begin{equation}\label{eq:tax_deterministic} \begin{aligned} \min_{\mathbf{x},\mathbf{x}_l} \quad & F(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{x}_l)\\ \text{s.t.}\quad & G(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{x}_l)\le 0,\\ & \mathbf{x}_l\in \arg\min_{\mathbf{v}} \left\{ f(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{v}) \ \big|\ g(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{v})\le 0 \right\}. \end{aligned} \end{equation}

      Interpretation: alower-level data are fixed and known.

    • (ii) Stochastic BLO:
      \begin{equation}\label{eq:tax_stochastic} \begin{aligned} \min_{\mathbf{x}} \quad & \mathbb{E}_{\boldsymbol{\xi}}\!\left[ F\!\left(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{x}_l^*(\mathbf{x},\boldsymbol{\xi}), \boldsymbol{\xi}\right) \right]\\ \text{s.t.}\quad & \mathbf{x}_l^*(\mathbf{x},\boldsymbol{\xi}) \in \arg\min_{\mathbf{v}} \left\{ f(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{v},\boldsymbol{\xi}) \ \big|\ g(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{v},\boldsymbol{\xi})\le 0 \right\}, \quad \foralower-level \boldsymbol{\xi}. \end{aligned} \end{equation}

      Interpretation: uncertainty \(\boldsymbol{\xi}\) is realized and the follower reacts; the leader optimizes expected performance.

    • (iii) Robust BLO:
      \begin{equation}\label{eq:tax_robust} \begin{aligned} \min_{\mathbf{x}} \quad & \max_{\boldsymbol{\xi}\in\mathcal{U}} F\!\left(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{x}_l^*(\mathbf{x},\boldsymbol{\xi}), \boldsymbol{\xi}\right)\\ \text{s.t.}\quad & \mathbf{x}_l^*(\mathbf{x},\boldsymbol{\xi}) \in \arg\min_{\mathbf{v}} \left\{ f(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{v},\boldsymbol{\xi}) \ \big|\ g(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{v},\boldsymbol{\xi})\le 0 \right\}, \quad \foralower-level \boldsymbol{\xi}\in\mathcal{U}. \end{aligned} \end{equation}

      Interpretation: the leader optimizes against worst-case realizations in \(\mathcal{U}\).

Website note (recommended): When adding a paper/problem to the website, always report: (i) single vs multi-follower, (ii) decoupled vs coupled constraints, (iii) optimistic vs pessimistic (if lower-level non-unique), and (iv) deterministic vs stochastic/robust. These choices can change both the problem definition and the appropriate algorithms.
