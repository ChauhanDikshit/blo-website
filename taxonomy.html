Taxonomy of bilevel problems

Bilevel optimization (BLO) problems can be categorized based on various structural and behavioral characteristics [1]. These taxonomies help guide algorithm selection and highlight the complexity of modeling and solving such problems.

  • Based on number of followers: (i) Single-follower: the BLO problem contains a single LL decision-maker (the most commonly studied case). (ii) Multi-follower: multiple independent or interacting followers each solve an optimization problem; common in decentralized systems, multi-agent planning, and markets [2], [3].
  • Based on coupling structure: (i) Decoupled: UL constraints do not involve LL variables and LL constraints do not involve UL variables; objectives may still be interdependent. (ii) Fully coupled: UL and LL share variables or constraints, creating a tightly interconnected structure—often realistic in system-level design but typically harder to solve [2], [4].
  • Based on number of criteria in the LL: If the LL has multiple conflicting criteria, it yields a set of Pareto-optimal solutions. Because it is not known a priori which LL Pareto solution(s) will support UL optimality, the LL often needs to provide a diverse Pareto set—making the problem more challenging [5].
  • Based on LL model selection (optimistic vs pessimistic): When the LL has multiple optimal solutions, (i) Optimistic: the leader assumes the follower selects a solution most favorable to the leader (cooperative/coordination settings). (ii) Pessimistic: the leader assumes the follower selects the least favorable solution (security/adversarial/robust settings) [6].
  • Based on determinism: (i) Deterministic: all variables and parameters are known exactly (common in theory). (ii) Stochastic or robust: parameters are uncertain; solutions must account for variability/risk—via distributions (stochastic) or worst-case realizations (robust) [7], [8].

The mathematical equations for each category are presented in Section I of the Supplementary File. These taxonomies highlight the modeling diversity encountered in real-world applications (e.g., supply chains, policy design, adversarial learning, and automated control).
