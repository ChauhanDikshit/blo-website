Taxonomy of bilevel problems (with equations)

Bilevel optimization (BLO) problems can be categorized using structural and behavioral characteristics [1]. This taxonomy is useful for (i) selecting algorithms, (ii) anticipating computational difficulty, and (iii) reporting modeling assumptions (e.g., optimistic vs pessimistic) clearly.

  • Based on number of followers: This category specifies how many lower-level (LL) decision-makers respond to a single upper-level (UL) leader.
    • (i) Single-follower BLO: A single follower solves one LL optimization problem in response to the leader:
      \begin{equation}\label{eq:tax_single_follower} \begin{aligned} \min_{\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y}} \quad & F(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y})\\ \text{s.t.}\quad & G(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y})\le 0,\\ & \mathbf{y}\in \arg\min_{\mathbf{v}} \left\{ f(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{v}) \ \big|\ g(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{v})\le 0 \right\}. \end{aligned} \end{equation}

      Interpretation: the leader anticipates the follower’s best response \(\mathbf{y}^*(\mathbf{x})\) (or a set \(S(\mathbf{x})\) if non-unique).
      Where it appears: leader–follower games, pricing, interdiction, and ML tasks (e.g., hyperparameter tuning as UL and training as LL).
      Method implications: KKT/duality or differentiable BLO is often viable when LL is convex/smooth; nested or derivative-free methods are used for discrete/nonconvex/black-box LL.

    • (ii) Multi-follower BLO: Multiple followers solve LL problems, either independently or with interactions (markets, decentralized control, multi-agent systems) [2], [3].

      Independent followers (separable LLs):

      \begin{equation}\label{eq:tax_multi_follower_ind} \begin{aligned} \min_{\mathbf{x}} \quad & F(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y}_1,\ldots,\mathbf{y}_m)\\ \text{s.t.}\quad & \mathbf{y}_i \in \arg\min_{\mathbf{v}_i} \left\{ f_i(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{v}_i)\ \big|\ g_i(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{v}_i)\le 0 \right\}, \quad i=1,\ldots,m . \end{aligned} \end{equation}

      Interpretation: each follower reacts separately; evaluation typically requires \(m\) LL solves per UL candidate.

      Interacting followers (Generalized Nash / equilibrium-type LL):

      \begin{equation}\label{eq:tax_multi_follower_int} \mathbf{y}_i \in \arg\min_{\mathbf{v}_i\in \mathbf{Y}_i(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y}_{-i})} f_i(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{v}_i,\mathbf{y}_{-i}) \quad \text{s.t.}\quad g_i(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{v}_i,\mathbf{y}_{-i})\le 0, \quad i=1,\ldots,m, \end{equation}

      where \(\mathbf{y}_{-i}=(\mathbf{y}_1,\ldots,\mathbf{y}_{i-1},\mathbf{y}_{i+1},\ldots,\mathbf{y}_m)\).
      Interpretation: the LL outcome is an equilibrium concept rather than independent optima.
      Method implications: decomposition/penalty/equilibrium reformulations are common; uniqueness/existence assumptions matter more.

  • Based on coupling structure: Coupling describes how UL and LL constraints depend on each other’s variables, strongly influencing tractability.
    • (i) Decoupled constraints: UL constraints involve only \(\mathbf{x}\) and LL constraints involve only \(\mathbf{y}\), while objectives may remain coupled:
      \begin{equation}\label{eq:tax_decoupled} \begin{aligned} \min_{\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y}} \quad & F(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y})\\ \text{s.t.}\quad & G(\mathbf{x})\le 0,\\ & \mathbf{y}\in \arg\min_{\mathbf{v}} \left\{ f(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{v})\ \big|\ g(\mathbf{v})\le 0 \right\}. \end{aligned} \end{equation}

      Interpretation: feasibility sets are structurally simpler, but bilevel difficulty persists via the LL optimality mapping.
      Method implications: LL feasibility does not change with \(\mathbf{x}\); however \(\mathbf{x}\mapsto \mathbf{y}^*(\mathbf{x})\) can still be nonsmooth or set-valued.

    • (ii) Fully coupled constraints: UL and LL constraints share variables (the most general and often most realistic case) [2], [4]:
      \begin{equation}\label{eq:tax_fully_coupled} \begin{aligned} \min_{\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y}} \quad & F(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y})\\ \text{s.t.}\quad & G(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y})\le 0,\\ & \mathbf{y}\in \arg\min_{\mathbf{v}} \left\{ f(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{v})\ \big|\ g(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{v})\le 0 \right\}. \end{aligned} \end{equation}

      Interpretation: \(\mathbf{x}\) can alter LL feasibility and the LL optimum set; regime switches are common.
      Method implications: KKT/MPEC reformulations often lead to complementarity constraints; discrete/nonconvex LL usually requires specialized or heuristic approaches.

  • Based on number of criteria in LL (multiobjective LL): If the LL has multiple conflicting objectives, the LL “solution” becomes a Pareto-optimal set [5].
    \begin{equation}\label{eq:tax_multiobj_LL} \begin{aligned} \min_{\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y}} \quad & F(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y})\\ \text{s.t.}\quad & \mathbf{y}\in \mathcal{P}(\mathbf{x}) := \left\{ \mathbf{y}\in\mathbf{Y}\ \big|\ \mathbf{y}\ \text{is Pareto-optimal for}\ \min_{\mathbf{v}\in\mathbf{Y}} \ \mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{v})\right\}. \end{aligned} \end{equation}

    Interpretation: the LL reaction is a set \(\mathcal{P}(\mathbf{x})\); a selection rule is needed to evaluate the UL objective.
    Why challenging: it is not known a priori which LL Pareto points induce UL optimality; algorithms may need a diverse LL Pareto approximation.
    Method implications: must define (and report) LL preference/selection (scalarization, reference point, representative set, etc.).

  • Based on LL model selection (tie-breaking): When the LL has multiple optimal solutions, the modeling choice defines tie-breaking. Let \[ S(\mathbf{x}) := \arg\min_{\mathbf{v}} \left\{ f(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{v}) \ \big|\ g(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{v})\le 0 \right\}. \]
    • (i) Optimistic bilevel:
      \begin{equation}\label{eq:tax_optimistic} \begin{aligned} \min_{\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y}} \quad & F(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y})\\ \text{s.t.}\quad & G(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y})\le 0,\\ & \mathbf{y}\in S(\mathbf{x}). \end{aligned} \end{equation}

      Interpretation: if \(S(\mathbf{x})\) is multi-valued, the follower selects an LL-optimal \(\mathbf{y}\) most favorable to the leader.
      Method implications: often more tractable; widely used in benchmarks and algorithm development.

    • (ii) Pessimistic bilevel:
      \begin{equation}\label{eq:tax_pessimistic} \begin{aligned} \min_{\mathbf{x}} \ \max_{\mathbf{y}\in S(\mathbf{x})} \quad & F(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y})\\ \text{s.t.}\quad & \mathbf{x}\in \mathbf{X}. \end{aligned} \end{equation}

      Interpretation: the leader plans against the worst LL-optimal response [6].
      Method implications: typically harder; solutions may be sensitive to how the inner maximization over \(S(\mathbf{x})\) is computed.

  • Based on determinism (deterministic / stochastic / robust): This category specifies whether parameters are known exactly or uncertain [7], [8].
    • (i) Deterministic BLO:
      \begin{equation}\label{eq:tax_deterministic} \begin{aligned} \min_{\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y}} \quad & F(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y})\\ \text{s.t.}\quad & G(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y})\le 0,\\ & \mathbf{y}\in \arg\min_{\mathbf{v}} \left\{ f(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{v}) \ \big|\ g(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{v})\le 0 \right\}. \end{aligned} \end{equation}

      Interpretation: all data are fixed and known.

    • (ii) Stochastic BLO:
      \begin{equation}\label{eq:tax_stochastic} \begin{aligned} \min_{\mathbf{x}} \quad & \mathbb{E}_{\boldsymbol{\xi}}\!\left[ F\!\left(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}^*(\mathbf{x},\boldsymbol{\xi}), \boldsymbol{\xi}\right) \right]\\ \text{s.t.}\quad & \mathbf{y}^*(\mathbf{x},\boldsymbol{\xi}) \in \arg\min_{\mathbf{v}} \left\{ f(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{v},\boldsymbol{\xi}) \ \big|\ g(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{v},\boldsymbol{\xi})\le 0 \right\}, \quad \forall \boldsymbol{\xi}. \end{aligned} \end{equation}

      Interpretation: uncertainty \(\boldsymbol{\xi}\) is realized and the follower reacts; the leader optimizes expected performance.

    • (iii) Robust BLO:
      \begin{equation}\label{eq:tax_robust} \begin{aligned} \min_{\mathbf{x}} \quad & \max_{\boldsymbol{\xi}\in\mathcal{U}} F\!\left(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}^*(\mathbf{x},\boldsymbol{\xi}), \boldsymbol{\xi}\right)\\ \text{s.t.}\quad & \mathbf{y}^*(\mathbf{x},\boldsymbol{\xi}) \in \arg\min_{\mathbf{v}} \left\{ f(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{v},\boldsymbol{\xi}) \ \big|\ g(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{v},\boldsymbol{\xi})\le 0 \right\}, \quad \forall \boldsymbol{\xi}\in\mathcal{U}. \end{aligned} \end{equation}

      Interpretation: the leader optimizes against worst-case realizations in \(\mathcal{U}\).

Website note (recommended): When adding a paper/problem to the website, always report: (i) single vs multi-follower, (ii) decoupled vs coupled constraints, (iii) optimistic vs pessimistic (if LL non-unique), and (iv) deterministic vs stochastic/robust. These choices can change both the problem definition and the appropriate algorithms.
