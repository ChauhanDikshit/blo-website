Fundamentals of BLO

Bilevel optimization (BLO) problems are a class of hierarchical mathematical programs where one optimization task is nested within another. The decision-making process is split into two levels: an upper-level (UL, leader) problem and a lower-level (LL, follower) problem. The key distinction from classical constrained optimization is that the lower-level problem is not a constraint in the traditional sense. It is itself an optimization problem whose solution depends on the upper-level decisions [1], [2].

Single-objective bilevel problems

At its core, BLO is a mathematical program where an optimization problem functions as a constraint for another optimization problem [3]. The leader optimizes its objective, subject to constraints that include the follower’s best possible reaction.

Below, Eq. \(\eqref{eq:svblo}\) is a standard single-objective bilevel form (often studied due to analytical tractability for certain subclasses).

\begin{align} &\min_{\mathbf{x}_u \in \mathbf{x}_u,\, \mathbf{x}_l \in \mathbf{x}_l} \, F(\mathbf{x}_u, \mathbf{x}_l) \nonumber\\ &\text{s.t.}\begin{cases} G_i(\mathbf{x}_u,\mathbf{x}_l) \le 0,\quad i \in \{1,2,\ldots,M\},\\ \mathbf{x}_l \in \arg\!\min_{\mathbf{x}_l \in \mathbf{x}_l} \Big\{ f(\mathbf{x}_u, \mathbf{x}_l) \ \big|\ g_j(\mathbf{x}_u, \mathbf{x}_l) \le 0,\ j \in \{1,2,\ldots,m\} \Big\}.\end{cases}\label{eq:svblo} \end{align}

where

  • \(\mathbf{x}_u \in \mathbb{R}^{n_{x_u}}\): upper-level decision variables; \(\mathbf{x}_l \in \mathbb{R}^{n_{x_l}}\): lower-level decision variables;
  • \(F:\mathbb{R}^{n_{x_u}}\times\mathbb{R}^{n_{x_l}}\to\mathbb{R}\): upper-level objective; \(f:\mathbb{R}^{n_{x_u}}\times\mathbb{R}^{n_{x_l}}\to\mathbb{R}\): lower-level objective;
  • \(G_i:\mathbb{R}^{n_{x_u}}\times\mathbb{R}^{n_{x_l}}\to\mathbb{R}\): upper-level inequality constraints; \(g_j:\mathbb{R}^{n_{x_u}}\times\mathbb{R}^{n_{x_l}}\to\mathbb{R}\): lower-level inequality constraints;
  • \(\mathbf{X}_u \subseteq \mathbb{R}^{n_{x_u}}\), \(\mathbf{X}_l \subseteq \mathbb{R}^{n_{x_l}}\): feasible domains (can be continuous, integer, or mixed-integer) [4].
  • Note: Equality constraints are often omitted in the generic form for readability, but they can be included at either level without changing the basic structure.

The upper-level decision-maker optimizes \(F\) through \(\mathbf{x}_u\), anticipating that the lower-level reacts optimally by minimizing \(f\). The feasible set of the upper-level is implicitly constrained by the optimal response mapping of the LL, which can make the problem nonconvex, nonsmooth, and even discontinuous. Example: Toll setting in transportation systems

A government chooses toll values \(\mathbf{x}\) to maximize toll revenue \(F(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y})\), where \(\mathbf{y}\) denotes the resulting traffic flow. Travelers respond by selecting routes that minimize their travel cost \(f(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y})\) (including tolls and travel time). The UL models revenue maximization, while the LL captures user equilibrium/route choice under network constraints. This illustrates the nested leader–follower structure typical of BLO. [1]

Multi-objective bilevel problems

Multi-objective bilevel optimization (MOBLO) extends classical BLO by introducing multiple objectives at the UL, LL, or both. This hierarchical multi-objective structure increases complexity: the follower’s response mapping becomes set-valued, and the leader must reason about trade-offs within and across levels [5].

A common MOBLO formulation is shown in Eq. \(\eqref{eq:moblo}\).

\begin{equation} \begin{aligned} \min_{\mathbf{x}_u \in \mathbf{x}_u,\, \mathbf{x}_l \in \mathbf{x}_l} \ & \mathbf{F}(\mathbf{x}_u,\mathbf{x}_l) = \big(F_1(\mathbf{x}_u,\mathbf{x}_l), \ldots, F_K(\mathbf{x}_u,\mathbf{x}_l)\big) \\ \text{s.t.}\quad & G_i(\mathbf{x}_u, \mathbf{x}_l)\le 0,\quad i\in\{1,2,\ldots,M\},\\ & \mathbf{x}_l \in \arg\min_{\mathbf{x}_l \in \mathbf{x}_l} \Big\{\mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x}_u,\mathbf{x}_l) = \big(f_1(\mathbf{x}_u,\mathbf{x}_l), \ldots, f_k(\mathbf{x}_u,\mathbf{x}_l) \big)\ \big| \\ & \qquad\qquad\qquad\qquad g_j(\mathbf{x}_u, \mathbf{x}_l)\le 0,\quad j\in\{1,2,\ldots,m\}\Big\}. \end{aligned} \label{eq:moblo} \end{equation}
  • \(\mathbf{F}: \mathbf{x}_u\times\mathbf{x}_l\to\mathbb{R}^K\), \(K\ge 2\) (multi-objective UL);
  • \(\mathbf{f}: \mathbf{x}_u\times\mathbf{x}_l\to\mathbb{R}^k\), \(k\ge 1\) (lower-level may be single- or multi-objective);
  • Special case: SVBLO if \(K=1\) and \(k\ge 2\) (upper-level single objective, lower-level multi-objective).

Different MOBLO definitions may impose additional requirements (e.g., multiple constraints), treating SVBLO as a special case [6].

Example in text: “We use the MOBLO formulation in Eq. \(\eqref{eq:moblo}\) to model leader trade-offs under a set-valued follower response.”
