Quick definition

Bilevel programs were first studied by Bracken and McGill [1], motivated by applications in military planning as well as production and marketing decision making. At the time, these models were often described as mathematical programs with optimization problems in the constraints. The terms bilevel optimization programming and multilevel optimization programming were introduced later by Candler and Norton [2].

Bilevel optimization (BLO) problems have a hierarchical structure in which an upper-level optimization problem is constrained by the optimal response of a lower-level optimization problem (Eq. \eqref{eq: blo}) [3]. Such models arise naturally in settings with interdependent decisions across levels of authority or operation, including leader–follower games, pricing and interdiction, network and infrastructure design, and modern machine learning pipelines such as hyperparameter tuning and meta-learning. The defining nested structure makes BLO challenging: feasibility depends on lower-level optimality, and the induced feasible set is typically nonconvex. It can be disconnected, and solution quality can be sensitive to the multiplicity of lower-level solutions and tie-breaking.

\begin{align}\label{eq: blo} & \min_{x_u}\! F(x_u, x_l), \\& \begin{cases} \text{s.t. }\; G(x_u, x_l)\le 0, \\ x_l \in \arg\!\min_{x_l}\{ f(x_u, x_l): g(x_u, x_l)\le 0 \} \end{cases} \end{align}

where

  • \(x_u\): upper-level (leader) decision vector;
  • \(x_l\): lower-level (follower) decision vector;
  • \(F, G\): leader objective and constraints;
  • \(f, g\): follower objective and constraints;
  • \(\arg\!\min\) makes the lower-level optimum set a constraint on \((x_u, x_l)\);
  • Note: Equality constraints are often omitted in the generic form for readability, but they can be included at either level without changing the basic structure.

Over the past decades, BLO has been studied from both theoretical and applied perspectives. Classical approaches exploit analytical structure through techniques such as Karush-Kuhn-Tucker (KKT)-based reformulations, penalty and value-function methods, and exact algorithms, such as branch-and-bound. While these methods can be effective for structured instances (e.g., smooth or convex lower-level problems), their applicability is often limited in practice by nonsmoothness, nonconvexity, discrete variables, or the lack of reliable gradients, especially when the lower-level problem is non-differentiable or only accessible through a black-box solver.

To address these limitations, the evolutionary computation community has developed metaheuristic and population-based approaches for BLO. Evolutionary algorithms (EAs) are a branch of metaheuristic algorithms, including genetic algorithms (GA), differential evolution (DE), particle swarm optimization (PSO), and co-evolutionary strategies, which are attractive for complex, multimodal, and black-box settings because they are derivative-free and can explore diverse regions of the search space. However, their flexibility comes at a cost: EAs often require many function evaluations, and the nested evaluation of lower-level optima can make them computationally expensive when lower-level solves are costly [4].

More recently, BLO has seen rapid growth at the intersection with machine learning (ML) [5]. Enabled by larger datasets, advances in automatic differentiation, and the need for scalable optimization pipelines, ML-based BLO methods include surrogate modeling (supervised learning), reinforcement learning for policy-level bilevel tasks, and deep learning–driven formulations for neural architecture search and hyperparameter optimization. In particular, differentiable bilevel frameworks, via implicit differentiation or unrolled optimization, can provide efficient gradient-based updates across both levels for suitable problem classes, substantially improving scalability and practical performance.

This nested structure makes BLO difficult: nonconvexity, nonsmoothness, implicit constraints, and multiple local/global follower optima.

Why is BLO hard?

  • Nested optimization: Feasibility depends on lower-level optimality, not just constraints. This creates an implicit “induced region” defined through the follower’s solution mapping. [1], [6].
  • Nonconvexity & irregular geometry: Even when the subproblems look simple, the induced region and the resulting upper-level landscape are often nonconvex and can become nonsmooth or irregular. [3].
  • Multiplicity & tie-breaking: The follower may have multiple optimal responses; results depend on how ties are resolved (optimistic vs pessimistic bilevel semantics), and this issue becomes even more prominent in multiobjective settings [4], [7].
  • Expensive evaluations: Many practical algorithms “nest” lower-level solves inside upper-level search, so each upper-level candidate can trigger one (or many) follower optimizations—making the overall procedure computationally expensive [4], [7].
  • Hardness in theory: Even restricted bilevel classes are computationally difficult; general bilevel programs (e.g., linear–linear cases) can reach high complexity (e.g., NP-hard and beyond in standard complexity hierarchies) [8], [9].

Common categories

  • Variable types: continuous, integer, or mixed-integer decisions at the upper and/or lower level (MIBLP).
  • Follower structure: linear/convex vs nonconvex; smooth vs nonsmooth; unique vs multiple follower optima.
  • Solution concepts: optimistic vs pessimistic bilevel; single-follower vs multi-follower; deterministic vs stochastic/robust variants.
  • Reformulation-friendly vs black-box: some problems admit single-level reductions (KKT/MPEC, strong duality, value function), while others require derivative-free or simulation-based treatment.
  • Objectives: single-objective BLO and multiobjective BLO (Pareto leader and/or Pareto follower).
