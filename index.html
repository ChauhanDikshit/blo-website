Quick definition

Bilevel optimization (BLO) problems have a hierarchical structure in which an upper-level (UL) optimization problem is constrained by the optimal response of a lower-level (LL) optimization problem [1]. Such models arise naturally in settings with interdependent decisions across levels of authority or operation, including leader–follower games, pricing and interdiction, network and infrastructure design, and modern machine learning pipelines such as hyperparameter tuning and meta-learning. The defining nested structure makes BLO challenging: feasibility depends on LL optimality, and the induced feasible set is typically nonconvex. It can be disconnected, and solution quality can be sensitive to the multiplicity of LL solutions and tie-breaking.

Over the past decades, BLO has been studied from both theoretical and applied perspectives. Classical approaches exploit analytical structure through techniques such as Karush-Kuhn-Tucker (KKT)-based reformulations, penalty and value-function methods, and exact algorithms, such as branch-and-bound. While these methods can be effective for structured instances (e.g., smooth or convex LL problems), their applicability is often limited in practice by nonsmoothness, nonconvexity, discrete variables, or the lack of reliable gradients, especially when the LL problem is non-differentiable or only accessible through a black-box solver.

To address these limitations, the evolutionary computation community has developed metaheuristic and population-based approaches for BLO. Evolutionary algorithms (EAs) are a branch of metaheuristic algorithms, including genetic algorithms (GA), differential evolution (DE), particle swarm optimization (PSO), and co-evolutionary strategies, which are attractive for complex, multimodal, and black-box settings because they are derivative-free and can explore diverse regions of the search space. However, their flexibility comes at a cost: EAs often require many function evaluations, and the nested evaluation of LL optima can make them computationally expensive when LL solves are costly [2].

More recently, BLO has seen rapid growth at the intersection with machine learning (ML) [3]. Enabled by larger datasets, advances in automatic differentiation, and the need for scalable optimization pipelines, ML-based BLO methods include surrogate modeling (supervised learning), reinforcement learning for policy-level bilevel tasks, and deep learning–driven formulations for neural architecture search and hyperparameter optimization. In particular, differentiable bilevel frameworks, via implicit differentiation or unrolled optimization, can provide efficient gradient-based updates across both levels for suitable problem classes, substantially improving scalability and practical performance.

\begin{align} & \min_{x_u}\! F(x_u, x_l), \\& \begin{cases} \text{s.t. }\; G(x_u, x_l)\le 0, \\ x_l \in \arg\!\min_{x_l}\{ f(x_u, x_l): g(x_u, x_l)\le 0 \} \end{cases} \end{align}

where

  • \(x_u\): upper-level (leader) decision vector;
  • \(x_l\): lower-level (follower) decision vector;
  • \(F, G\): leader objective and constraints;
  • \(f, g\): follower objective and constraints;
  • \(\arg\!\min\) makes the LL optimum set a constraint on \((x_u, x_l)\).

Note: Equality constraints are often omitted in the generic form for readability, but they can be included at either level without changing the basic structure.

This nested structure makes BLO difficult: nonconvexity, nonsmoothness, implicit constraints, and multiple local/global follower optima.

Why is BLO hard?

  • Nested optimization creates an implicit feasible region.
  • Nonconvexity & discontinuity appear even with simple pieces.
  • Multiplicity: follower may have multiple optima (pessimistic/optimistic).
  • Computational complexity is often high (frequently NP-hard / Σp2-hard in general settings).

Common categories

  • Continuous / integer / mixed-integer BLO
  • Linear / convex / nonconvex followers
  • Single-level reduction (KKT, value function, strong duality)
  • Multi-objective BLO (Pareto leader + nested follower)
