Quick definition

Bilevel optimization (BLO) problems have a hierarchical structure in which an upper-level (UL) optimization problem is constrained by the optimal response of a lower-level (LL) optimization problem \cite{colson2007overview}. Such models arise naturally in settings with interdependent decisions across levels of authority or operation, including leader–follower games, pricing and interdiction, network and infrastructure design, and modern machine learning pipelines such as hyperparameter tuning and meta-learning. The defining nested structure makes BLO challenging: feasibility depends on LL optimality, the induced feasible set is typically nonconvex and can be disconnected, and solution quality can be sensitive to LL solution multiplicity and tie-breaking. A bilevel optimization problem has a leader (upper level) decision whose feasible set is constrained by the optimal response of a follower (lower level).

minimizex F(x, y)
subject to y ∈ argminy { f(x, y) : g(x, y) ≤ 0 }, X constraints

This nested structure makes BLO difficult: nonconvexity, nonsmoothness, implicit constraints, and multiple local/global follower optima.

Why is BLO hard?

  • Nested optimization creates an implicit feasible region.
  • Nonconvexity & discontinuity appear even with simple pieces.
  • Multiplicity: follower may have multiple optima (pessimistic/optimistic).
  • Computational complexity is often high (frequently NP-hard / Σp2-hard in general settings).

Common categories

  • Continuous / integer / mixed-integer BLO
  • Linear / convex / nonconvex followers
  • Single-level reduction (KKT, value function, strong duality)
  • Multi-objective BLO (Pareto leader + nested follower)
